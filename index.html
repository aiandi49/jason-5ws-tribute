WINDSOR, CONNECTICUT — The 5 W's Podcast, Episode Release

The 5 W's released its most personal sitting yet this week — a two-part episode built around a single name. Co-host Erica "Sis" Myers answered the same five questions the show puts to every subject — who, what, where, when, and why — about the boy she met at fourteen, the man she loved on and off for thirty years, and the friend she lost, suddenly, two years ago. What came out of it wasn't really an interview. It was a testimony.

Jason

"Jason was, is, and always will be the love of my life."
— Erica "Sis" Myers

Some episodes explain a person. This one resurrects one. The Dickies matched top and bottom, one color for every mood. The braids he swore he'd never cut — and never did. A porch in Windsor where two teenagers could sit for an hour and say maybe twelve words. Recorded across several sittings, Erica walks the show's full five-question frame around a man named Jason: who he was before the world found him, and who he became after.

Who

The Boy on the Porch

Cool, Calm, Collected

Before anything else, Jason had a look — and he knew it. Bone Thugs-n-Harmony ran his whole aesthetic: the big afro, later braided or twisted to match the group, and a closet built almost entirely out of two-piece Dickies sets, shirt and khaki pants dyed to match, one color for practically every day of the week. He was light-skinned, sharp-featured, and aware enough of it to be a little insecure about things that made no logical sense to anyone looking at him.

He was sweet with his mother, easy with his father, and something close to a mama's boy underneath the swagger. He didn't take well to being told what to do. He could be finicky, could be difficult — and could, somehow, still be gorgeous while doing it. What set him off was stupidity, and anyone who so much as looked at Erica sideways. He wanted to fight the whole world on her behalf, for reasons she still can't fully explain.

"He had this flair to him — like he was walking on a cloud. It wasn't that he exuded confidence. The confidence was just: I'm Jason. I'm me. I'm chill."

Erica, on Jason as a teenager

They lived down the street from each other their entire lives without meeting until their teenage years — a fact Erica still finds a little funny. Once they found each other, the porch became a kind of headquarters: hours of watching cars pass, saying almost nothing, needing to say almost nothing.

What

What He Gave, What He Chased

Showing Up, Every Time

Ask Erica what Jason did, and the answer isn't a résumé — it's a pattern. He was there. First apartment, first move out of state, the first night alone in a New Jersey apartment with no roommates: Jason showed up and stayed. "You're going to follow me your whole life," she used to tell him. He did.

What made her mad was the opposite instinct — a reluctance to apply himself that she read, for years, as laziness. It took hindsight to recognize it as something heavier. Jason went through a serious depression, and Erica admits she initially waved it off: he was so good-looking, what did he have to be depressed about? He never cut his hair — not once, not ever, a promise he made as a young man and kept until the day he died — but toward the end, he stopped being able to even comb it. The same guy who wouldn't leave the house without every detail in place stopped caring what he looked like at all.

"He told me a long time ago — I'm never cutting my hair. And to the day he died, he never did."

Erica

His favorite song was "Ordinary People" by John Legend — Erica believes because it felt like them. He wanted, someday, to be an electrician, though she admits she never once saw him touch a wire; she thinks the confidence to chase it simply got buried. What he did do, constantly, was document things — flowers, cars, sunsets, whatever the day handed them, camera always close. And when nothing else fit the moment, he had one word for it, delivered flat: "Nope."

When

1994 to 2024

Love At First Sight, Thirty Years Later

Erica can name the exact season. September 1994, a birthday party thrown by twin friends that wasn't really a party at all — just a group of teenagers hanging out until the older boys came through. She clocked Jason on sight and decided he looked like he belonged in Bone Thugs-n-Harmony, a group she'd only just started listening to. There was one wrinkle: word was he already had a girlfriend, a girl from Erica's own middle school. Erica's read on that was immediate. We're going to have to fix that.

"It was literally love at first sight — September of 1994."

Erica, on meeting Jason

The fixing took a minute. A friend passed along a phone number for Jason that turned out to be wrong, and when Erica's calls went nowhere, she figured the moment had passed her by. It hadn't. Jason later told her he'd stayed in the house for three days straight, waiting on a call that was never going to come, before their paths crossed again and the mix-up got sorted out. After that, there was no more losing touch: daily phone calls, her father driving her to his house after school, hours spent on his porch "like old people," watching cars go by. His own mother reportedly told him for years — y'all are so cute together.

By the time Erica met him, Jason was already, in her words, more grown man than kid — trusted with real freedom, held to a real curfew, treated by his parents like someone who'd earned both. The two of them talked early about a future together, even about a big family, but made a shared, deliberate choice not to rush into parenthood as teenagers. Whatever else changed between them over the next thirty years, that first certainty never fully left. 1994 to 2024 — the two dates Erica reaches for when asked how long it lasted.

Where

Windsor to the Tri-State, and Back

A Love Story in Geography

Erica believes Jason is somewhere better now — heaven, paradise, "whatever we call it" — and she visits often, if only in sleep. She describes the dreams less like memory and more like travel: teleporting to wherever he is. Two years before he passed, the two of them spent four straight hours at a picnic table by the Connecticut River in Windsor, saying very little of consequence, until Erica asked what he was going to do with himself. "Nothing," he said. She didn't know it yet, but that was the moment he'd already let go.

The map of their life together runs Windsor to Brooklyn to New Jersey — him riding the train down just to sit in her apartment, making his own friends in her building, the two of them heading into the city to take pictures at Branch Brook Park, then pulling over at a high point to photograph the sunset over Manhattan.

"At his funeral, they played a slideshow off his Facebook page. Ninety percent of those photos were ones he took at my house. I don't think anyone in that room ever knew that."

Erica

Not every chapter of the map is easy. Erica left for college at eighteen, frustrated that Jason wasn't building toward anything. She came back for him anyway — and found him in the park with a young woman who was pregnant with his child. She never told him she'd come back for him. She made a quiet decision that day that whatever they were, it wasn't going to be. Still, she says, if they'd had a child of their own, the crib would've sat right next to their bed — Jason wanted a "brood," six kids deep, and he half-convinced her of it too.

Why

The Weight He Carried

Handled Gently, Told Honestly

Jason told Erica he was introduced to alcohol around age fifteen, by a friend's mother. What started, in his words, as something they were "just doing" for fun crept from a beer here and there to forty-ounces to liquor. Underneath that was something older: at thirteen, in the middle of an argument, his father told him that's why you're adopted — a line Jason carried, unresolved, for the rest of his life. He didn't look like his siblings. He favored his mother, who had passed. He never once asked her directly, and never got the chance to.

"I don't believe Jason didn't love himself. I think he was just disappointed in his choices."

Erica

Their breakup at eighteen — Erica leaving for school, Jason telling her afterward that he'd simply stopped caring about anything — seems to mark the turn. The drinking got heavier. The self-care disappeared. He developed COPD, which Erica believes ultimately took him. She doesn't frame his death as a story of a man who failed to love himself, but of a man who ran out of road: "It was just time for Jason to go. He didn't need to be here anymore." Two things she wants remembered above all — he never stopped being kind, and he never stopped being loved.

The Message

In one of the first dreams Erica had after he passed, Jason kept calling her — on a phone that wasn't quite a phone, in a world that wasn't quite this one — and kept missing her. When the message finally got through, secondhand, it was simple: he was desperately sorry. Not for anything in particular, Erica thinks, so much as for giving up too soon.

"Jason was my best friend, my love, the love of my life, and my all-around everything."

Erica, in conclusion

The host closes the episode with his own two cents — a smaller, quieter memory. In his father's final week, Jason showed up, cooked, and stayed close, making a hard stretch easier for a man he didn't have to do that for. Erica remembers him watching hours of the host's old drumming performances on tape, rewinding one part ten times over, grinning the whole way through.

"He made dad comfortable, even if just for a second. I love him forever and a day for that. He's in the right place, at the right time. Dad's up there with him too — they're playing music right now, who knows. Jason was awesome. We love you, man."

About The 5 W's

The 5 W's is a memory-keeping podcast that asks one structured set of questions — who, what, where, when, and why — about a single life, guiding the people left behind through grief one question at a time. Each episode is built as a teaching tool as much as a tribute: a format anyone can borrow to sit with their own Ericas, their own Jasons, and get the stories down before they're gone.

Grief doesn't move in a straight line, and neither does this episode. If anything here sits heavy — depression, alcohol, loss — you don't have to carry it alone. The SAMHSA National Helpline (1-800-662-4357) is free, confidential, and available 24/7.
